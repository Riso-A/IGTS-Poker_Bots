PART B
SCENARIO 1: Everyone prefers even the smallest stag share (1/n) over a hare
CASE 1: Everyone hunts Stag 
Everyone gets a 1/n share, which is strictly preferred over a hare. No one wants to switch to Hare and get less. Nash Equilibrium.
CASE 2: Partial cooperation 
Stag hunters get a large share 1/s and stay. However, hare hunters can switch to Stag, increase the hunting group to s+1, and secure a stag share (>=1/n) that beats a hare. No Equilibrium.
CASE 3: Not enough hunters to catch the stag 
The stag is not caught, giving stag hunters 0. They will actively deviate to Hare for a guaranteed payoff. No Equilibrium.
CASE 4: 0 people hunt Stag (s = 0)
Everyone hunts Hare. If a single person switches to Stag, they cannot catch it alone and get 0, which is worse than a hare. No one deviates. Nash Equilibrium.
SCENARIO 2: Players prefer a hare over any stag share smaller than 1/k
CASE 1: Over-crowded hunt (s > k)
The stag group is too large, diluting individual shares below 1/k. Since players prefer a hare over this small fraction, stag hunters will actively drop out to hunt Hare. No Equilibrium.
CASE 2: Exactly k people hunt Stag (s = k)
Stag hunters get exactly 1/k and stay. If a hare hunter joins, the group grows to k+1, dropping the payoff below a hare's value. No one moves. Nash Equilibrium.
CASE 3: Moderate cooperation 
Hare hunters will actively switch to Stag because adding themselves to the group results in a size s+1 <= k, which still guarantees a stag share worth more than a hare. No Equilibrium.
CASE 4: Fewer than m people hunt Stag 
The stag is not captured. Stag hunters get 0 and will switch to Hare. No Equilibrium.
CASE 5: 0 people hunt stag 
Everyone hunts Hare. A lone deviation to Stag yields 0, which is worse than a hare. Nobody deviates. Nash Equilibrium
